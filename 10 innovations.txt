THE 10 INNOVATIONS — what must be invented, and why (clean text)

Legend: each entry = the innovation needed, what exists today, and why it is the blocker.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. In-vivo, non-destructive barcode readout
   Today: readout ends in sequencing (destructive, kills the tissue) or surface optics
          (blind below the cortex).
   Why:   Door #1. You cannot upload a living person by slicing their brain. Without reading
          molecular barcodes deep and alive, nothing else matters.

2. Massively-multiplexed reporters (thousands of distinguishable channels per voxel)
   Today: about 3 to 8 distinguishable channels (gas-vesicle collapse pressures, MRI
          relaxation bins).
   Why:   A deep voxel holds around 1,000,000 synapses. Sensors cannot resolve them by
          position (the physics wall) — only by identity. The capacity law says a human fUS
          voxel needs about 46 bits (roughly 7e13 codewords). This is the multiplexing wall.

3. Trans-synaptic pairing at scale and fidelity (SYNseq-class)
   Today: early, noisy, high false-pair rate, small scale.
   Why:   Barcodes label neurons (the nodes). To recover the wiring you must read which
          barcode pairs with which across each synapse (the edges), at 1e14 synapses with low
          error. Without it you have a parts list, not a circuit.

4. About 100 percent neuron delivery (BBB-crossing vector)
   Today: AAV-PHP.eB crosses the blood-brain barrier and transduces many neurons in mice, but
          not all, and not proven in humans.
   Why:   Any unlabeled neuron is a coverage gap, which is a blind spot. "Most neurons" is not
          a complete connectome.

5. Signal-boost and noise reduction at depth (SNR)
   Today: fUS and MRI reach deep; reporters are faint; the skull aberrates the beam.
   Why:   Sets how deep you can read before the signal drowns, which creates blind spots
          (Layer A). Mostly won by brighter, more specific reporters (molecular) plus
          skull-aberration correction, not a bigger scanner.

6. Whole-brain scan throughput and time
   Today: per-voxel dwell plus averaging.
   Why:   Cutting noise means averaging longer, but 1e14 synapses times exabytes makes total
          scan time its own wall. Needs parallel readout and faster schemes.

7. Exabyte-scale assembly and error-correction
   Today: works at mouse-scale projectomes.
   Why:   Turning noisy reads back into a graph at human scale is a data and algorithms problem
          (consensus, GNN gap-fill, confidence) — exabytes, not gigabytes.

8. Whole-brain twin simulation
   Today: runs worm to mouse; v1 simulates connectomes.
   Why:   The recovered map only counts if you can run it. Scaling the twin to 86 billion
          neurons and 1e14 synapses is its own compute frontier.

9. Behavioural upload-verification protocol
   Today: v2 scores worm behavioural fidelity in simulation.
   Why:   You must prove the twin behaves like the original, not just that edges overlap, and
          define what "successfully uploaded a person" even means.

10. Human safety of the whole chain
    Today: AAV and FUS-BBB-opening tested in limited human trials.
    Why:   Delivery, reporter expression, and wave exposure must be reversible and safe in a
           living human before any of this is ethical.
