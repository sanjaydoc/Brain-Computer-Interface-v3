50 INVENTION PROMPTS TO TRY — BCI v3 cockpit
Ten categories, five variants each. Every variant chases the SAME goal for its category but
from a different creative lens, so you get diverse designs. Paste a prompt into the cockpit,
pick the matching topic and the suggested lens, and hit Invent + Simulate. Add "aggressive"
to push the parameters harder. (Lens diversity really shows once a local LLM is wired via
bci serve; the browser proposer is deterministic.)

==============================================================================
CATEGORY 1 — In-vivo, non-destructive barcode readout
Goal: read molecular barcodes ~75 mm deep in a LIVING brain, without harming tissue.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1 (lens: biomimicry)
    Copy how deep-diving or echolocating animals sense structure through dense tissue, and
    turn that mechanism into a safe deep reader for molecular barcodes.
1.2 (lens: crossDomain)
    Borrow from seismic imaging and medical sonar: use phase-corrected focused ultrasound plus
    gas-vesicle reporters to read barcodes 75 mm deep without a biopsy.
1.3 (lens: inversion)
    Stop trying to extract the molecule. Make each barcode actively broadcast a safe signal
    outward, so a coarse external sensor can hear it from deep in living tissue.
1.4 (lens: extreme)
    Describe the perfect lossless deep reader, then find the simplest real approximation using
    today's ultrasound or MRI reporters that stays under all safety limits.
1.5 (lens: combinatorial)
    Fuse focused ultrasound, engineered acoustic reporters, and skull-aberration correction
    into one non-destructive reader that works at depth.

CATEGORY 2 — Massively-multiplexed reporters
Goal: give a human fUS voxel enough distinguishable channels to tell ~1,000,000 synapses apart
by identity. Maximize barcode bits.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 (lens: combinatorial)
    Combine several orthogonal reporter families so their signatures multiply into thousands of
    distinguishable channels per voxel; push barcode bits past 46.
2.2 (lens: scaling)
    Scale the codebook 1000x: engineer reporters whose collapse pressure or relaxation time
    spans many fine, separable bins, so identity carries the resolution.
2.3 (lens: analogical)
    Treat it like DNA sequencing colours or QR codes: design a reporter alphabet with a huge,
    reliably-readable codespace for one voxel.
2.4 (lens: crossDomain)
    Import frequency-division multiplexing from radio: assign each barcode a distinct spectral
    slot the sensor can demodulate inside a crowded voxel.
2.5 (lens: reduction)
    Find the minimum reporter set that still reaches ~46 bits of identity, so the design is
    simple enough to actually express in every neuron.

==============================================================================
CATEGORY 3 — Trans-synaptic pairing at scale and fidelity
Goal: read which pre-barcode pairs with which post-barcode across every synapse, low error,
edge F1 above 0.9.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1 (lens: analogical)
    Copy how contact tracing links two identities from a brief encounter; apply it to pairing
    pre and post barcodes at each synapse with high fidelity.
3.2 (lens: historical)
    Study why early SYNseq pairing was noisy, then design a scheme that specifically suppresses
    false pairs and dropout to clear F1 = 0.9.
3.3 (lens: combinatorial)
    Merge trans-synaptic barcode transfer with molecular consensus tags so each true synapse is
    read many times and one-off false pairs are rejected.
3.4 (lens: extreme)
    Assume perfect pairing chemistry, then approximate it with realistic transfer efficiency and
    enough sequencing depth to hold edge fidelity high.
3.5 (lens: inversion)
    Instead of pairing at the synapse, tag both partners with a shared random token the moment
    they connect, then recover edges by matching tokens.

CATEGORY 4 — About 100 percent neuron delivery
Goal: label essentially every neuron with a BBB-crossing vector, no coverage blind spots, dose
safely below the toxicity ceiling.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.1 (lens: crossDomain)
    Borrow targeting tricks from immunology and drug delivery to reach every neuron class with a
    vector, while keeping the viral dose safe.
4.2 (lens: biomimicry)
    Mimic a neurotropic virus that naturally reaches the whole brain, but engineer it to be safe
    and to transduce all neuron types.
4.3 (lens: combinatorial)
    Combine an engineered BBB-crossing capsid with focused-ultrasound BBB opening so coverage
    approaches 100 percent at a lower systemic dose.
4.4 (lens: scaling)
    Scale delivery from mouse to human brain volume without leaving blind spots; solve the
    dose-versus-coverage trade so no neuron is missed.
4.5 (lens: reduction)
    Find the smallest, safest delivery recipe that still hits every neuron once, minimizing the
    viral load needed for full coverage.

CATEGORY 5 — Signal-boost and noise reduction at depth (SNR)
Goal: keep the read working at 75 mm depth by raising signal and cutting noise, not by a bigger
scanner.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.1 (lens: inversion)
    Do not build a stronger scanner. Make the source brighter and the background quieter so the
    same sensor reads clearly at depth.
5.2 (lens: biomimicry)
    Borrow signal-amplification from bioluminescence cascades to make reporters far brighter, so
    SNR survives the trip out of deep tissue.
5.3 (lens: combinatorial)
    Fuse brighter reporters, heavy averaging, and skull-aberration correction into one SNR
    budget that clears detection at 75 mm.
5.4 (lens: extreme)
    Assume an ideal noiseless channel, then approximate it with real averaging and background
    subtraction that still reads at full depth.
5.5 (lens: reduction)
    Strip the SNR problem to its core term and win it with the single most effective lever:
    reporter brightness. Keep everything else minimal.

CATEGORY 6 — Whole-brain scan throughput and time
Goal: read the whole brain in under 30 days via parallel readout and short per-voxel dwell.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.1 (lens: scaling)
    Scale readout channels massively in parallel so total scan time drops from years to under a
    month; trade dwell time for parallelism.
6.2 (lens: crossDomain)
    Borrow from data-center parallelism and camera sensor arrays: read thousands of voxels at
    once instead of sweeping one at a time.
6.3 (lens: extreme)
    Describe an instantaneous whole-brain read, then find the most parallel real design that
    finishes inside the 30-day budget.
6.4 (lens: reduction)
    Cut the scan to its essential operation and parallelize only that, so throughput hits the
    target with minimal hardware complexity.
6.5 (lens: future)
    Assume 2050 readout hardware exists; work back to the parallel-channel count and dwell time
    needed today to reach a sub-30-day scan.

CATEGORY 7 — Exabyte-scale assembly and error-correction
Goal: assemble a human connectome from noisy reads within a few exabytes, residual error under
5 percent.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.1 (lens: reduction)
    Reduce assembly to a minimal consensus-plus-confidence pipeline that fits a few exabytes and
    holds residual error under 5 percent.
7.2 (lens: crossDomain)
    Borrow from genome assembly and error-correcting codes to reconstruct the wiring graph from
    noisy reads at exabyte scale.
7.3 (lens: combinatorial)
    Combine consensus voting, GNN gap-fill, and per-edge confidence so the assembled graph is
    both accurate and storage-efficient.
7.4 (lens: scaling)
    Scale a mouse-projectome assembler to 1e14 synapses without the data or error blowing up;
    keep storage bounded and error low.
7.5 (lens: future)
    Assume mature exascale data tooling; work back to the read format and redundancy needed now
    to make human-scale assembly tractable.

CATEGORY 8 — Whole-brain twin simulation
Goal: run an 86-billion-neuron twin at real time (real-time factor at least 1).
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8.1 (lens: future)
    Assume zettascale compute is available; design the lean per-synapse kernels needed to run
    the full twin at real time.
8.2 (lens: scaling)
    Scale v1's connectome simulator from worm to 86 billion neurons while keeping the update
    O(edges) and hitting real time.
8.3 (lens: crossDomain)
    Borrow from large-scale graph processing and GPU sparse kernels to step 1e14 synapses fast
    enough for a real-time twin.
8.4 (lens: reduction)
    Reduce each neuron update to the cheapest faithful operation, so the whole-brain twin runs
    in real time on realistic hardware.
8.5 (lens: extreme)
    Describe an infinitely fast simulator, then find the most efficient real kernel and hardware
    mix that still reaches a real-time factor of one.

CATEGORY 9 — Behavioural upload-verification protocol
Goal: prove the recovered brain behaves like the original; behavioural fidelity above 0.9.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9.1 (lens: extreme)
    Design the most demanding possible battery of stimuli and a sensitive divergence metric that
    would expose any behavioural mismatch above chance.
9.2 (lens: analogical)
    Treat it like a Turing test or hardware regression suite: define pass criteria that certify
    the twin behaves like the original.
9.3 (lens: historical)
    Learn from failed model-validation efforts; design a protocol that avoids their false
    positives and holds fidelity above 0.9.
9.4 (lens: inversion)
    Instead of proving similarity, aggressively try to make the twin diverge; if it cannot, the
    upload is verified.
9.5 (lens: reduction)
    Find the smallest set of behaviours whose match is sufficient to certify a faithful upload,
    keeping the protocol simple but decisive.

CATEGORY 10 — Human safety of the whole chain
Goal: keep delivery, expression, and wave exposure inside every known safety limit with headroom.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10.1 (lens: historical)
     Learn from past AAV and focused-ultrasound adverse events; design a pipeline that stays well
     under intensity, mechanical-index, and viral-dose limits.
10.2 (lens: reduction)
     Reduce the safety problem to the three binding limits and keep generous headroom on each:
     ultrasound intensity, mechanical index, and viral dose.
10.3 (lens: inversion)
     Design for reversibility first: assume everything must be switchable off, then build the
     rest of the chain around that safety constraint.
10.4 (lens: crossDomain)
     Borrow safety-engineering practice from aviation and clinical trials to bound every risk in
     the mapping chain before any human use.
10.5 (lens: extreme)
     Assume zero tolerance for harm; find the safest still-workable settings for delivery,
     expression, and wave exposure.
