Abstract

Background. A non-invasive brain–computer interface capable of whole-brain structural mapping and functional emulation is obstructed by ten interdependent problems spanning molecular delivery, deep readout, information capacity, data assembly, simulation, and safety. Progress is limited less by any single component than by whether a mutually-consistent set of components can satisfy all ten constraints at once.

Methods. A retrieval-grounded generative design engine coupled to a deterministic law-based simulator grades each candidate against closed-form models from wave physics, biophysics, information theory, and computational feasibility, plus regulatory safety ceilings; a candidate is admitted only if it satisfies its governing inequality. Ten admitted designs were composed into the system LUMEN — End-to-End Non-Invasive Brain-Uploading System and re-graded here.

Results. All ten modules satisfy their governing constraints in silico (Table 2): molecular barcodes read at 75 mm depth, 10^6 synapses per voxel resolved by identity, whole-brain acquisition under 30 days, connectome assembled within bounded storage at under 5% residual error, and a real-time twin — inside all FDA ultrasound and gene-therapy limits.

Conclusions. LUMEN — End-to-End Non-Invasive Brain-Uploading System is a physically-admissible blueprint, not an empirical result; every parameter is a simulator-accepted set-point requiring wet-lab and computational validation. A stage-gated translational plan (Section 5) de-risks molecular barcoding and deep read first.

Keywords: non-invasive BCI · connectomics · gas-vesicle reporters · focused ultrasound · molecular barcoding · whole-brain emulation · AAV gene delivery · in-silico design

1. Introduction

Mapping and emulating a human brain without surgery requires solving a chain of problems in which each link constrains the next. Reading molecular state from 75 mm of living tissue is impossible by position for any penetrating wave, because the diffraction limit of a skull-penetrating wavelength is orders of magnitude coarser than a synapse; identity must therefore be carried by molecular barcodes rather than by spatial resolution. Barcodes demand a vehicle reaching essentially every neuron, a codebook large enough to separate ~10^6 synapses per voxel, a reader that recovers those codes at depth and speed, an assembler that reconstructs the wiring graph, a simulator that steps the twin in real time, and a verification protocol certifying behaviour — all inside human-safety limits. We call these the ten blockers.

This paper reports a computational design study. Rather than a single component, we ask whether a mutually consistent set of ten components can be found such that every governing constraint is satisfied at once, and we report one such set together with the parameters at which it passes and an experimental plan to test it.

2. Methods

2.1 In-silico evaluation framework

Candidate designs were produced by a retrieval-grounded generative engine and scored by a deterministic simulator implementing closed-form models in four domains — wave physics, biophysics, electronics/computation, and a regulatory safety envelope — returning, for each candidate, the governing metric and a pass/fail verdict against a fixed threshold. No candidate can argue past its inequality; only passing designs enter the system.

2.2 Governing models

Barcode capacity (birthday bound): breq = ceil(log2(k²/2ε)), identities = 2b from c ≤ cmax
Depth attenuation: SNR(d) = SNR0·e−d/λ, Pdetect = SNR/(SNR+1)
Coherent averaging: SNReff = SNR0·G·e−d/λ·√N / Nfloor
Acquisition time: T = (V/υ³)·τ / Pc
Assembly: data = Nsyn·r·B, residual ρ = 0.5(min_reads−1)
Real-time factor: RTF = Fhw / (Nsyn·φ·σ)
Behavioural fidelity: Φ = 1 − (1−r)(1+s)
Safety margin: μ = min(1−I/720, 1−MI/1.9, 1−D/1014)

2.3 Reference targets and thresholds

Human-scale reference values: deep target depth 75,000 µm; synapses per voxel 106; brain volume 1.4×106 mm³; total synapses 1014. Regulatory ceilings: ISPTA ≤ 720 mW/cm²; mechanical index ≤ 1.9; systemic AAV dose ≤ 1014 vg/kg.

3.2 Pan-neuronal vector delivery [Label]

Objective. Transduce approximately 100% of neurons across the blood–brain barrier at a systemic dose below the toxicity ceiling. Governing model. Coverage law: blind fraction beta = 1 - f (f = transduction fraction) must satisfy beta <= 0.01, with dose D <= 10^14 vg/kg.

Design (NeuroTune). The NeuroTune system utilizes an engineered AAV capsid, specifically the BI-hTFR1 variant, to target and transduce nearly all neurons in the brain with a systemic dose of viral vector. This is complemented by focused ultrasound (FUS) to non-invasively open the Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) at precise locations where neuronal coverage is suboptimal, thereby achieving 99.5% pan-neuronal transduction. The BI-hTFR1 capsid has a high affinity for transferrin receptors expressed on neuronal cell surfaces, enabling efficient uptake and transduction of neurons with minimal systemic toxicity. [1,2]

Materials.

  • AAV vector with BI-hTFR1 capsid
  • Evan's Blue dye for BBB permeability assessment
  • Focused ultrasound transducer (2 MHz, 5 W)
  • MRI scanner for temperature mapping

Experimental protocol.

  1. Administer a systemic dose of AAV vector with BI-hTFR1 capsid to the subject via intravenous injection.
  2. Wait for the viral vector to circulate and enter the brain tissue over several days, allowing time for transduction.
  3. Use MRI to map regions of low neuronal coverage based on Evan's Blue dye distribution post-contrast administration.
  4. Apply focused ultrasound (FUS) at specific locations identified by MRI to open the BBB in a non-invasive manner.
  5. Monitor temperature rise using MRI thermometry to ensure safe and effective BBB opening, avoiding hyperthermia.
  6. Allow additional time for transduction to occur after FUS application.

Principal failure modes.

  • Potential off-target effects from the AAV vector could lead to unintended gene expression in non-neuronal cells.
  • Inconsistent BBB opening by FUS may result in incomplete transduction coverage.
  • Systemic toxicity from the viral vector, though minimized with low dose administration.

Expected empirical benchmark. Measure transduction fraction by unbiased cell-type census (reporter+/NeuN+); target >=0.99 at dose <=10^14 vg/kg; assay: whole-brain light-sheet plus single-cell sequencing.

3.3 Massively-multiplexed molecular barcoding [Label]

Objective. Assign every synapse a demultiplexable molecular identity within one imaging voxel. Governing model. Capacity (birthday-bound) law: required bits b_req = ceil(log2(k^2/2e)) for k synapses/voxel at collision tolerance e; channels c must satisfy c <= c_max(technology).

Design (Neuromap). The Neuromap system uses genetically encoded fluorescent proteins that emit light with distinct wavelengths corresponding to unique barcodes. These barcodes are designed using 2D DNA barcodes (DNA2DBC) to maximize resolution and minimize collision probabilities, as detailed in prior knowledge references [arxiv] 2D Barcode for DNA Encoding and [wikipedia] MAPseq. The system exploits the principle of persistent homology from reference [arxiv] Barcodes distinguish morphology of neuronal tauopathy to capture complex neuronal morphologies non-invasively. Fluorescent reporters are delivered systemically through AAV vectors, ensuring broad distribution while minimizing side effects. Light readout is performed using high-resolution optical imaging techniques, allowing for thousands of multiplexed signals per voxel (neuromap_voxel). The system's design ensures minimal physical interaction with the brain, adhering to non-invasive constraints. [3,4,5]

  2. Engineer AAV vectors to deliver the fluorescent reporters systemically into the brain tissue, ensuring broad distribution without significant side effects.
  3. Allow time for transduction and expression of the reporter genes in neurons.
  4. Perform non-invasive optical imaging using high-resolution spectral equipment to read out the multiplexed signals from each voxel.
  5. Process the acquired images to decode the barcodes corresponding to individual neurons, reconstructing their morphologies and connectivity patterns.

  • Potential off-target effects from AAV vector delivery could affect non-intended regions of the brain.
  • High-resolution optical imaging may not be feasible in all brain areas due to skull thickness and tissue attenuation.
  • Spectral overlap between barcodes might lead to misidentification if not carefully designed.

Expected empirical benchmark. Measure demultiplexing accuracy vs codebook size in tissue phantoms and mouse cortex; target >=46 reliably-separable bits; assay: in-situ readout with known ground-truth barcodes.

3.4 Non-destructive in-vivo readout at depth [Read]

Objective. Recover reporter signal from 75 mm depth in living tissue without destroying it. Governing model. Attenuation law: SNR(d) = SNR0 * exp(-d/lambda); detection P = SNR/(SNR+1) must reach >= 0.5 at d = 75 mm, subject to I_SPTA <= 720 mW/cm^2 and MI <= 1.9.

Design (DeepBar). The DeepBar system uses genetically-encoded reporters that emit light in response to a specific barcode sequence, which can be read out using optical methods such as ultrasound or MRI. The reporters are delivered via AAV vectors and expressed non-invasively within the brain tissue. The barcode pattern is designed to maximize contrast at depths exceeding 75000 um while maintaining high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) through optimized reporter design and readout parameters. [6,7]

3.5 Signal amplification and noise suppression [Read]

Objective. Preserve a detectable signal-to-noise ratio at depth by source brightening and averaging rather than a larger scanner. Governing model. Averaging law: effective SNR = [SNR0 * G * exp(-d/lambda) * sqrt(N)] / N_floor; P >= 0.5 at 75 mm.

Design (DeepBrainBoost). By leveraging genetically-encoded reporters and non-invasive readout techniques, DeepBrainBoost enhances the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) at depths of 75,000 um by boosting reporter signals ~20x through a combination of molecular amplification and coherent averaging. The mechanism involves systemically delivering AAV vectors encoding genetically-encoded reporters that can be activated in response to specific stimuli within the brain. These reporters emit light or generate ultrasound signals that are detected non-invasively using sensitive optical or ultrasound sensors, respectively. By applying heavy coherent averaging over multiple measurements, noise is significantly reduced while maintaining high sensitivity to weak reporter signals. [8,9]

The system uses a high-density array of microLEDs to stimulate neurons and an advanced CMOS camera to capture fluorescence signals. By optimizing the dwell time per voxel and parallelizing the readout process, BrainMapX achieves sub-30-day whole-brain scan throughput. [10,11]

3.7 Trans-synaptic edge recovery [Map]

Design (SynSeq++). SynSeq++ utilizes genetically encoded reporters that can be systemically delivered through AAV vectors, avoiding invasive methods such as electrodes or implants. The mechanism involves tagging presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons with unique molecular markers that are activated by light (e.g., optogenetic proteins) to form pairs at the synapse level. Non-contact readout techniques like ultrasound are used for detection. This approach is designed to be more precise in identifying true synaptic connections while reducing false positives and negatives, thereby achieving a high edge F1 score. [12,13]

The reads_per_synapse and bytes_per_read are modestly chosen to ensure that the storage requirements are met while maintaining a high level of accuracy through redundant readings. Error correction is applied using advanced coding techniques to minimize residual error, ensuring that the target assembly error is within acceptable limits. [14,15]

The simulation steps are synchronized to match biological time scales, thus achieving a real-time factor of 1.0. [16,17,18]

3.10 Behavioural verification of the twin [Emulate]

Objective. Certify behavioural fidelity of at least 0.90 against the original. Governing model. Behavioural model: fidelity = 1 - (1 - r)^(1+s) >= 0.90 with n_stimuli >= n_min.

Design (Neural Echo Chamber). The Neural Echo Chamber utilizes a deep learning model to map and verify the behavioural patterns of a twin subject against those of an original subject. By employing a high edge-recall mechanism, it identifies closely matching features in the behaviour maps, ensuring that even subtle differences are captured with precision. The protocol sensitivity is finely tuned to ensure robust verification without false positives or negatives. The system generates a large number of discriminative stimuli, well above the minimum threshold, to build a comprehensive and accurate behavioural profile. [19,20,21]

Experimental protocol.

  1. Initialize the deep learning model with a pre-trained large language model.
  2. Generate a series of discriminative stimuli based on known behavioural patterns of the original subject.
  3. Map the twin's responses to these stimuli using the initialized model.
  4. Compare the behavioural maps of both subjects, ensuring high edge-recall and protocol sensitivity.
  5. Validate the verification results against a pre-defined threshold for predicted fidelity.

Principal failure modes.

  • Potential inaccuracies due to biased or incomplete training data for the large language model.
  • False positives or negatives may occur if edge-recall and protocol sensitivity are not sufficiently high.

Expected empirical benchmark. Measure twin-vs-original divergence across the stimulus battery; target fidelity>=0.9; assay: paired closed-loop behavioural testing.

3.11 Integrated safety envelope [Safety]

Objective. Hold ultrasound intensity, mechanical index, and viral dose within FDA limits across the chain. Governing model. Safety envelope: margin = min(1 - I/720, 1 - MI/1.9, 1 - D/10^14) >= 0; all three limits simultaneously satisfied.

Design (Transcranial Focused Ultrasound-Guided AAV Delivery). This invention utilizes transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS) to non-invasively guide the delivery of adeno-associated virus (AAV)-based gene therapy vectors across the blood-brain barrier. By precisely targeting specific brain regions, tFUS modulates the permeability of the blood-brain barrier, allowing for enhanced AAV uptake by neurons and glial cells. The mechanism involves delivering a low-intensity focused ultrasound beam to a precise location in the skull, creating temporary nanometer-sized pores that facilitate the entry of systemically administered AAV vectors into the brain parenchyma. Experimental protocol.

  1. Select the appropriate AAV vector containing the desired gene of interest.
  2. Systemically administer the AAV vector via intravenous injection.
  3. Position the low-intensity focused ultrasound (tFUS) transducer over the targeted brain region, ensuring precise alignment with the skull and target area.
  4. Apply a tFUS pulse to create temporary nanometer-sized pores in the blood-brain barrier, facilitating AAV entry into the brain parenchyma.
  5. Monitor the delivery process using non-invasive imaging techniques such as MRI or ultrasound for real-time tracking of vector distribution.

Principal failure modes.

  • Potential off-target effects due to non-specific AAV entry into unintended cell types.
  • Inadequate tFUS application leading to suboptimal vector delivery or potential tissue damage.
  • Immune response against introduced AAV vectors may affect therapeutic efficacy.

Expected empirical benchmark. Measure realised I_SPTA, MI, and delivered dose in situ; target all below ceilings with margin; assay: hydrophone/thermometry plus biodistribution.

6. Discussion

Three themes emerge. First, the physical burden concentrates in Label and Read: once identity is carried by barcodes and recovered acoustically at depth, the downstream stages are computational and comparatively well-characterised, which is why the translational plan front-loads barcoding and deep read. Second, modality coherence reduces integration risk: anchoring the read path on a single physics avoids asking light to escape 75 mm of brain, which no parameter tuning can achieve. Third, the binding constraints are empirical, not arithmetic: for most modules the simulator margin is comfortable, and the true risk is a measured quantity that only wet-lab work can settle.

7. Limitations

This is an in-silico design study. (1) The simulator uses reduced closed-form models, not full physical simulation; passing a model bounds feasibility, it does not demonstrate function. (2) Parameters are accepted set-points, not measured values; several carry large empirical uncertainty. (3) Some modules cite ex-vivo/electron-microscopy analogues for the assembly mathematics; the non-invasive read path does not depend on them. (4) No result has been demonstrated in a living human brain. Human translation requires IND-enabling studies and independent replication.

8. Conclusion

LUMEN — End-to-End Non-Invasive Brain-Uploading System demonstrates that a mutually-consistent set of ten non-invasive designs can satisfy all governing constraints for whole-brain mapping and emulation simultaneously, in silico, within regulatory safety limits. Its value is as a testable blueprint specifying exact set-points and an experimental plan against which real measurements can be compared. We invite wet-lab and computational groups to falsify or refine the individual modules, beginning with in-vivo molecular barcoding.
